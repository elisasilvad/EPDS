The Journey of
Art Ninfee Monalisa Guernica

Art moves around the world. The fact is, we do not know that.

We are used to think that works of art are immovable, closely related to the institution that preserves them, in such an extend, that we often memorize them almost in an affinity with that place.

What would we think if, for example, Monnalisa was moved from the Louvre?

Although the transfer of this masterpiece, an ensign of the museum (if not even of an entire country!), may seem an extraordinary event, the movement of artworks is actually a common practice, and indeed natural for the life of art and art institutions themselves.

The aim of this project is therefore to bring forward a new perspective on art as ‘something that moves’ .

PROVENANCE

In order to approach the question, this is the watchword, that refers to the history of the ownership of a work of art, and is able to describe the sequence of all the owners an artwork has had during its life. Provenance is therefore a litmus test of the journeys that works of art make from institution to institution, from city to city: it is the passport on which each work stamps its passage to a place in the world

A journey into the journeys of art

And so, to restore to art its essence as a moving thing, we started our journey on the trail of the journeys of works of art.
Moving from one part of the world to another, artworks leave traces. Footprints of their passage imprinted in archives, catalogs, collections of works.

Our journey has place in those left in one of the largest collection of Italian Art:

STOP 1: COUNTRIES

In order to discover the traces that Italian works of art leave in their path, we first had to see their direction around the world. According to the data that we examined, outside Italy but within the borders of Europe, there are up about to 425 pieces, while outside the borders of Europe, more than 1770 pieces are being hosted from several institutions, with the main country-keeper being USA.

STOP 2: CITIES

Indeed, the nationwide mobility of works of art is a broad perspective. What is even more interesting is that this movement is translated through a smaller-scale import and export of art speciments. STOP 3: INSTITUTIONS

However, import and exprot dynamic cannot be examined without a close up in the institutions and their contribution to the feild.



As was to be expected, Italy turns out to be the largest exporter of Italian paintings due to the exchange acts of its institutions. Among the 20 that sell Italian paintings, there are as many as 12 Italian entities, public and private, with the absolute dominance on the market of the Private Collection, which stands out among all the others for the large amount of paintings sold over time.



But the same thing can be said, surprisingly enough, about acquisitions as well. 10 Italian institutions are among the 20 largest acquirings of Italian art in the world.



Nonetheless, even the differences between the two acts is important.






Provenance makes necessary to rethink about instituions as well. They pass to be the place where art is to elements in a larger network.
